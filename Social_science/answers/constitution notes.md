# Constitution Section — Answers

## 1. Define Constitution, Law, Act, and Fundamental Rights

**Constitution:**
A Constitution is the fundamental set of rules, principles, and laws according to which a state or organization is governed. It is the **supreme law of the land** that defines the structure of government, the powers of its organs, and the rights and duties of its citizens.


- **Aristotle:** "Constitution is the way of life the state has chosen for itself




**Law:**
Law is a set of rules and regulations enforced by the state or a recognized authority to regulate the behavior of citizens and maintain order in society. Laws protect rights, ensure justice, and promote public welfare. Major types include constitutional law, criminal law, civil law, and administrative law.

**Act:**
An Act is a specific written law passed by the legislature (Parliament/Congress) and assented to by the head of state. It is also called a **statute**. While "law" is a broader concept, an "Act" is a formal legislative enactment dealing with a particular subject (e.g., the Right to Information Act, 2009).

**Fundamental Rights:**
Fundamental Rights are basic human rights guaranteed and protected by the constitution of a country. They are considered essential for the development of personality, liberty, and dignity of citizens. In Bangladesh, Articles 27–44 of the Constitution guarantee fundamental rights such as equality before law, freedom of speech, freedom of religion, right to life, and protection of law. They are enforceable by the High Court Division under Article 44.

## 2. Classifications of Constitution

Constitutions are classified mainly in the following ways:

### A. On the basis of form (Written / Unwritten):

1. **Written Constitution:** A consciously drafted document prepared by a constituent assembly. It is codified in a single document.
   *Examples:* USA, India, Bangladesh.

2. **Unwritten Constitution:** Grows gradually through customs, traditions, judicial decisions, and statutes; not contained in a single document.
   *Example:* United Kingdom.

### B. On the basis of amendment procedure (Bryce's classification):

3. **Rigid Constitution:** Cannot be amended by ordinary legislative process; requires a special procedure (e.g., two-thirds majority).
   *Examples:* USA, Japan, Switzerland, Bangladesh.

4. **Flexible Constitution:** Can be amended by the same procedure as ordinary law.
   *Example:* United Kingdom.

### C. On the basis of structure of government:

5. **Unitary Constitution:** All powers concentrated in the central government.
   *Examples:* UK, France, Bangladesh.

6. **Federal Constitution:** Powers divided between the central government and the states/provinces.
   *Examples:* USA, Canada, India.

### D. On the basis of the form of government:

7. **Parliamentary Constitution:** Executive is accountable to the legislature; PM heads the government.
   *Examples:* UK, India, Bangladesh.

8. **Presidential Constitution:** Executive is separate from and independent of the legislature.
   *Example:* USA.

### E. On the basis of origin:

9. **Evolved Constitution** – Develops gradually (e.g., UK).
10. **Enacted Constitution** – Deliberately drafted and adopted (e.g., USA, Bangladesh).

## 3. Characteristics of a Suitable Constitution for Any Country

A good or suitable constitution should possess the following characteristics:

1. **Written and clear:** A written constitution avoids confusion. According to K.C. Wheare, "One essential characteristic of the ideally best form of constitution is that it should be as short as possible."

2. **Brief and comprehensive:** It should cover all key areas of governance without going into unnecessary detail. (Prof. Getel: *"A constitution should cover the whole field of government."*)

3. **Clarity and simplicity:** It must be easily understandable to ordinary citizens.

4. **Flexibility with rigidity:** It should be rigid enough to remain stable but flexible enough to be amended when needed.

5. **Stability and durability:** A constitution should last for generations and not change frequently.

6. **Protection of fundamental rights:** Must guarantee civil, political, economic, and social rights.

7. **Separation of powers:** Clear demarcation among the executive, legislature, and judiciary.

8. **Independent judiciary:** Essential for protecting rights and upholding rule of law.

9. **Rule of law:** All citizens — including rulers — must be equal before the law.

10. **Supremacy of the constitution:** It must be the highest law; all other laws must conform to it.

11. **Adaptability:** Should be capable of accommodating change as society evolves.

12. **Acceptability:** Must reflect the values, culture, history, and aspirations of the people.

13. **Provision for amendment:** A proper, defined method to amend it should be included.

14. **Democratic character:** Should ensure popular sovereignty and citizen participation.

15. **Preservation of national unity and integrity.**

## 4. Nature of the Constitution of Bangladesh

The Constitution of Bangladesh was adopted on **4 November 1972** and came into effect on **16 December 1972**. Its nature can be described through the following features:

1. **Written constitution:** Contained in a single, codified document with a preamble, 11 parts, 153 articles, and schedules.

2. **Rigid constitution:** Amendment requires a **two-thirds majority** of the total members of Parliament (Article 142).

3. **Supreme law of the land:** Article 7 declares that the Constitution is the supreme law; any law inconsistent with it is void.

4. **Begins with "Bismillah-Ar-Rahman-Ar-Rahim"** in its preamble.

5. **Fundamental Principles of State Policy:** Includes nationalism, socialism, democracy, and secularism (the four state principles).

6. **Fundamental Rights:** A comprehensive list of fundamental rights is provided in Articles 27–44.

7. **Republican form of government:** Bangladesh is declared a People's Republic.

8. **Parliamentary form of government:** Prime Minister is the head of government; President is the head of state (largely ceremonial).

9. **Unitary form of government:** All powers concentrated in the central government.

10. **Unicameral legislature:** A single-chamber Parliament called the **Jatiya Sangsad** (National Parliament).

11. **Universal adult franchise:** Every citizen aged 18 and above has the right to vote.

12. **Independent judiciary:** Judiciary is separated from the executive (Article 22).

13. **Rule of law:** Equal protection of law for all citizens (Article 27).

14. **Citizenship:** Single citizenship for all citizens.

15. **Emergency provisions:** Provisions for declaring a state of emergency (Articles 141A–141C).

16. **Provision for amendment:** Constitution can be amended by Parliament under specific procedures.

## 5. Distinction Between Human Rights and Fundamental Rights

| **Aspect** | **Human Rights** | **Fundamental Rights** |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Definition** | Basic rights inherent to all human beings by virtue of being human. | Specific rights guaranteed and protected by the constitution of a country. |
| 2. **Source** | Derived from natural law, morality, and international declarations (e.g., UDHR, 1948). | Derived from the constitution of a particular state. |
| 3. **Scope** | Universal — apply to all human beings everywhere. | Limited to the citizens (and sometimes residents) of a specific country. |
| 4. **Nature** | Broader concept; includes civil, political, social, economic, and cultural rights. | Narrower; usually focuses on civil and political rights guaranteed in the constitution. |
| 5. **Enforcement** | Enforced through international bodies (UN, ICC) and treaties, often without strong legal force. | Enforced through national courts; in Bangladesh, by the High Court Division under Article 44/102. |
| 6. **Legal protection** | Protected by international conventions and treaties. | Protected by the supreme law of the land — the constitution. |
| 7. **Basic principle** | Right to life, liberty, and dignity. | Right to freedom and constitutional guarantees. |
| 8. **Suspension** | Cannot generally be taken away; considered inalienable. | May be suspended during a declared state of emergency. |
| 9. **Examples** | Right to life, freedom from torture, right against slavery, freedom of thought. | Equality before law, freedom of speech, freedom of religion, right to property. |
| 10. **Authority** | Created and maintained by global consensus and moral order. | Created and maintained by the state through its constitution. |

**In short:** All fundamental rights are human rights, but not all human rights are fundamental rights — only those incorporated into the constitution become fundamental rights.

## 6. Main Fundamental Rights of the Bangladesh Constitution

Articles **27 to 44** of the Constitution of Bangladesh guarantee **18 fundamental rights**. These are divided into:

- **Rights granted to all persons (citizens and non-citizens):** Articles 32, 33, 34, 35, 41, 44.
- **Rights granted only to citizens of Bangladesh:** Articles 27, 28, 29, 30, 31, 36, 37, 38, 39, 40, 42, 43.

### Main Fundamental Rights:

1. **Article 27 – Equality before law:** All citizens are equal before the law and entitled to equal protection of law.

2. **Article 28 – Non-discrimination:** State shall not discriminate on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Women shall have equal rights with men.

3. **Article 29 – Equality of opportunity in public employment:** Equal opportunity for all in government jobs.

4. **Article 30 – Prohibition of foreign titles:** No citizen may accept any foreign title without the President's approval.

5. **Article 31 – Right to protection of law:** Every citizen has the inalienable right to be treated only in accordance with law.

6. **Article 32 – Protection of right to life and personal liberty:** No person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty except by law.

7. **Article 33 – Safeguards as to arrest and detention:** Arrested persons must be informed of grounds, allowed legal counsel, and produced before a magistrate within 24 hours.

8. **Article 34 – Prohibition of forced labour:** All forms of forced labour are forbidden.

9. **Article 35 – Protection in respect of trial and punishment:** Right to fair trial; no double jeopardy; protection from torture and cruel treatment.

10. **Article 36 – Freedom of movement:** Right to move freely throughout Bangladesh.

11. **Article 37 – Freedom of assembly:** Right to assemble peacefully and without arms.

12. **Article 38 – Freedom of association:** Right to form associations or unions.

13. **Article 39 – Freedom of thought, conscience, and speech:** Includes freedom of the press.

14. **Article 40 – Freedom of profession or occupation:** Right to engage in any lawful profession or business.

15. **Article 41 – Freedom of religion:** Right to profess, practice, or propagate any religion.

16. **Article 42 – Right to property:** Right to acquire, hold, transfer, or dispose of property.

17. **Article 43 – Protection of home and correspondence:** Right to privacy of home and communication.

18. **Article 44 – Enforcement of fundamental rights:** Right to move the High Court Division for enforcement of these rights.

## 7. Obstacles Citizens Face in Ensuring Fundamental Rights

Although fundamental rights are guaranteed by the Constitution, citizens in Bangladesh face many obstacles in enjoying them in practice:

1. **Illiteracy and lack of awareness:** A large portion of the population is unaware of their constitutional rights and thus cannot claim them.

2. **Poverty:** Poor people cannot afford legal services or pursue justice; many sacrifice rights for survival.

3. **Corruption:** Widespread corruption in administration, police, and judiciary obstructs proper enforcement of rights.

4. **Political interference:** Political influence in administration and judiciary often denies justice to ordinary citizens.

5. **Misuse of power by law-enforcement agencies:** Unlawful arrest, custodial torture, and extrajudicial killings violate the right to life and liberty.

6. **Delay in justice:** Long backlogs of cases in courts mean justice is often delayed and effectively denied.

7. **Costly judicial process:** Legal procedures are expensive, putting fundamental rights out of reach of the poor.

8. **Lack of independent judiciary in practice:** Although the judiciary is constitutionally independent, executive influence often weakens it.

9. **Weak rule of law:** Laws are sometimes applied selectively, favoring the powerful.

10. **Restrictive laws:** Certain laws (e.g., Special Powers Act, Digital Security Act) are used to restrict freedom of speech, press, and assembly.

11. **Gender discrimination:** Despite Article 28, women face discrimination in social, economic, and family matters.

12. **Religious and ethnic discrimination:** Minorities sometimes face social and economic discrimination.

13. **Media restrictions:** Press freedom is sometimes curtailed by laws and political pressure.

14. **Emergency provisions:** Fundamental rights may be suspended during a declared state of emergency.

15. **Bureaucratic red-tapism:** Bureaucratic complexities and harassment hinder citizens from receiving services and protection.

16. **Lack of effective ombudsman:** Although the Constitution provides for an Ombudsman (Article 77), it has not been established in practice.

17. **Weak civil society and human rights organizations:** Limited capacity and political pressure restrict their effectiveness.

18. **Social conservatism and customs:** Patriarchal and conservative social norms restrict the rights of women and marginalized groups.

**Conclusion:** While the Constitution of Bangladesh guarantees broad fundamental rights, their actual enjoyment depends on the rule of law, an independent judiciary, accountable administration, political will, and an informed citizenry. Removing these obstacles is essential for making constitutional rights meaningful in real life.
