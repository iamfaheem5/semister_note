# Democracy Section — Solutions

## 1. Define Democracy

The word **democracy** comes from the Greek *demos* (people) and *kratos* (power/rule). It is a form of government in which sovereign power rests with the people, exercised either directly or through elected representatives.

**Key definitions:**
- **Abraham Lincoln:** "Democracy is a government of the people, by the people and for the people."

Thus, democracy is not merely a form of government but a way of life based on liberty, equality, and participation.

## 2. Difference Between Democracy and Autocracy (Dictatorship)

| Democracy | Autocracy / Dictatorship |
|---|---|
| 1. Emphasizes the **rights** of citizens. | 1. Emphasizes the **duties** of citizens. |
| 2. Based on **equality** of all citizens. | 2. Based on **hierarchy** and class. |
| 3. **Glorifies the individual** (man). | 3. **Glorifies the state**. |
| 4. Encourages **free thought and expression**. | 4. **Suppresses** free thought and action. |
| 5. Believes in **pacifism, debate and consensus**. | 5. Believes in **application of force**. |
| 6. Country governed by **the people** through representatives. | 6. Country controlled by **one person or party**. |
| 7. Leaders are **elected** through free and fair elections. | 7. The dictator **seizes power** or rules without genuine elections. |
| 8. **Rule of law** prevails; government is accountable. | 8. **Rule of the ruler**; no real accountability. |
| 9. Independent **judiciary, free press, opposition** exist. | 9. Judiciary, press, and opposition are **suppressed**. |
| 10. **Peaceful transfer of power** through elections. | 10. Power changes mostly through **revolt or coup**. |

## 3. Preconditions of Democracy

For democracy to function successfully, the following conditions must exist:

1. **Democratic-minded people** – Citizens who believe in democratic values. (Bryce: *"No government demands so much from citizens as democracy."*)
2. **Democratic atmosphere** – Protection of social, political, and economic rights.
3. **Spirit of brotherhood** – Cooperation, tolerance, and fraternity among citizens.
4. **Spread of education** – Educated citizens make informed choices.
5. **Economic independence** – Political freedom is meaningless without economic security.
6. **Decentralization of power** – Strong local government and balanced power distribution.
7. **Tolerance and persistence** – Willingness to compromise and accept opposing views.
8. **Active participation of people** – In voting, debate, and public affairs.
9. **Accountability** – Of politicians and bureaucrats at every level.
10. **Independent judiciary** – (Laski: *"Independence of the judiciary is essential for freedom and democracy."*)
11. **Free press and strong civil society**.
12. **Multi-party system and strong opposition**.

## 4. Merits and Demerits of Democracy

|**Merits (Advantages)**|**Demerits (Disadvantages)**|
|---|---|
|1. Safeguards interests of people – government is responsible to citizens.|1. Focus on quantity, not quality – majority may elect unfit leaders.|
|2. Based on equality – equal social, political, and legal rights.|2. Rule of incompetent/amateurs – equal vote for both wise and ignorant.|
|3. Stability and responsibility – fixed tenure and public consent.|3. Based on unnatural equality – treats unequals as equals.|
|4. Provides political education – through elections, debates, and parties.|4. Voter apathy – many citizens don't vote sincerely.|
|5. Reduces chances of revolution – peaceful change through ballots.|5. Lowers moral standards – use of money, muscle power, and character attacks.|
|6. Stable government – backed by public will.|6. Becomes rule of the rich (plutocracy) – elections need huge money.|
|7. Develops good citizenship – (Tocqueville: "Democracy is the first school of good citizenship.").|7. Wastes time and public money – slow laws and costly elections.|
|8. Based on public opinion – not on fear or force.|8. Unstable coalition governments – frequent collapse.|
|9. Protects fundamental rights and liberties.|9. Encourages party factionalism and corruption.|
|10. Encourages debate, dissent, and rational decisions.|10. Decisions get delayed due to long debates and consensus.|

## 5. Importance of Free and Fair Election for a Democratic Country

Free and fair elections are the **lifeblood of democracy**. Their importance includes:

1. **Expression of the people's will** – Elections allow citizens to choose who governs them; this is the essence of "government by the people."
2. **Legitimacy of government** – Only a government elected through free and fair elections is considered legitimate at home and abroad.
3. **Accountability of rulers** – Periodic elections force leaders to remain answerable to the public.
4. **Peaceful transfer of power** – Prevents revolts, coups, and political violence.
5. **Protection of fundamental rights** – A freely elected government is more likely to safeguard civil liberties.
6. **Ensures political equality** – "One person, one vote" guarantees equal political worth of every citizen.
7. **Develops political consciousness** – Election campaigns educate citizens about issues, policies, and parties.
8. **Encourages participation** – Citizens engage actively in the political process.
9. **Strong and stable government** – A government with a genuine mandate can govern effectively.
10. **Strengthens rule of law** – Elections conducted under a neutral commission uphold constitutionalism.
11. **Checks corruption and misuse of power** – Fear of losing elections discourages malpractice.
12. **Promotes good governance** – Transparent elections lead to transparent administration.
13. **Strengthens opposition** – A fair election gives opposition parties their rightful place, ensuring checks and balances.
14. **Maintains national unity** – When all groups feel represented, social harmony is preserved.
15. **International recognition** – Democracies with credible elections attract investment, aid, and diplomatic respect.

**Conclusion:** Without free and fair elections, democracy becomes a mere label. They are the foundation upon which all democratic institutions — parliament, executive, judiciary, and rule of law — ultimately rest.
