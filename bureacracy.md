**Bureaucracy**

Bureaucracy is the permanent system of government offices and officials that runs the daily work of the state. It is often called the “desk government.” It is made up of trained civil servants who are not elected but work under fixed rules and a clear chain of command to carry out laws and policies. Max Weber described it as a system based on expertness, hierarchy, and strict rules.

**Characteristics of Bureaucracy**

1. **Clear Hierarchy:** There is a firm top-to-bottom chain of command. Every official has a superior, and orders flow downward.
2. **Specialization:** Each worker has a fixed, specific job and usually becomes an expert in that area.
3. **Division of Labour:** Big tasks are split into small parts. Different people handle different parts so that work is done efficiently.
4. **Formal Rules:** Written rules and standard procedures guide every job. Everyone follows the same manual so that work is uniform and predictable.

**Role of Bureaucracy in Policy Formation in Bangladesh**

In Bangladesh, bureaucracy plays a major role in making and running government policies:

*   **Designing Policies:** Bureaucrats collect data and prepare projects, programs, and budgets according to the country’s needs.
*   **Planning:** They make detailed plans for things like hospitals, roads, and schools, checking local conditions and money limits.
*   **Advising Rulers:** Elected ministers are often not technical experts. Bureaucrats advise them on complex state affairs.
*   **Drafting Laws:** Many important bills that go to Parliament are actually written by bureaucrats.
*   **Implementation:** They turn paper plans into real work by building, distributing, and managing services.
*   **Link between People and Government:** They inform the public about government schemes and carry public complaints back to the government.
*   **Development and Stability:** They run development projects and act as mediators to keep peace among different groups.

**Accountability**

Accountability means that government officials must answer for their actions and decisions. If they have power, they must explain how they used it and accept blame or punishment if they do wrong. It has two simple steps: **answerability** (giving an explanation) and **enforcement** (facing action for mistakes).

**Transparency**

Transparency means that the government works in an open way. It shares information, rules, and decisions with the public so that citizens can easily see and understand what is happening. It helps people check whether the government is working properly.

**Problems to Achieve Accountability and Transparency in Administration**

Several problems stop accountability and transparency in Bangladesh:

1.  **Bureaucratic Resistance:** Officials often hide information and avoid being questioned because they want to protect their own power.
2.  **Distance from People:** Many bureaucrats keep themselves away from common people. They live and move in a separate, elite style, so ordinary citizens cannot easily reach them.
3.  **Political Interference:** Politics often mixes with administration. Ruling parties sometimes force officials to work for party interest instead of public interest.
4.  **Weak Parliamentary Committees:** The committees that should watch over officials are often too weak to control or question them.
5.  **Lack of Watchdogs:** For a long time, an independent office like the Ombudsman was missing, so there was no strong body to investigate public complaints against officials.
6.  **Not Following Rules:** Sometimes both business and government sectors do not follow clear rules, making it easy to hide mistakes and misuse power.
7.  **Elitist Attitude:** Some bureaucrats think they are rulers, not public servants, so they do not feel answerable to the people.
